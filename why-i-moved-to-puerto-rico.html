Why I Moved My Business to Puerto Rico — and Why More Founders Should Consider It

By Josh Bartch, Founder & CEO, MavDB Consulting LLC

People ask me about the move to Puerto Rico more than almost anything else. It's usually framed as a tax question — and yes, the tax benefits under Act 60 are real and significant. But if that's where the conversation stops, it misses most of what actually matters about building a business from the island.

Let me tell you what I've found.

The Act 60 Reality

I'll start with the taxes, because it would be dishonest not to. Puerto Rico's Act 60 program — the successor to the earlier Acts 20 and 22 — offers qualifying businesses and individuals meaningful tax advantages that are fully sanctioned by U.S. federal law. As a U.S. territory, Puerto Rico operates its own tax code, and for qualifying residents and businesses, the program offers rates on certain income categories that are simply not available anywhere else in a U.S. jurisdiction.

For a capital markets advisory firm like MavDB Consulting, the financial impact is material. Capital gains, passive income, and service income from international clients can all qualify for preferential treatment under different components of Act 60. This is not a gray area or an aggressive tax position — it is a deliberate policy choice by the Puerto Rico government to attract business investment and talent to the island.

But here's the thing: if the tax benefits were the only advantage, I might have stayed in the continental U.S. and found other ways to optimize. The taxes are a reason to look at Puerto Rico. They are not, by themselves, a reason to stay.

The Strategic Position

What I did not fully appreciate before moving was how valuable Puerto Rico's geographic and cultural position would be for the kind of work I do.

Capital markets advisory is a relationship business. It requires access to multiple worlds simultaneously — U.S. regulatory infrastructure, global investor networks, and increasingly, Latin American markets that are growing rapidly and generating the kind of companies that want U.S. market access. Puerto Rico sits at the intersection of all three.

San Juan is a fully U.S.-connected city. My banking relationships, my legal and accounting infrastructure, my access to U.S. institutional investors — all of that works exactly the same way it did when I was operating on the mainland. There is no offshore complexity, no currency conversion, no regulatory gap. You are in the United States. You just happen to be in the Caribbean.

At the same time, Puerto Rico's cultural position gives you a proximity to Latin American business culture that is genuinely valuable. I have been able to develop relationships with companies and investors across Latin America and the Caribbean that I would not have built as naturally from a desk in New York or Denver. That network has been a real asset for MavDB as we've expanded our practice.

What San Juan Is Actually Like

Before moving, I had a vague image of Puerto Rico that was shaped more by media coverage of the island's economic challenges than by direct experience. The reality of life and work in San Juan is very different from that image.

San Juan — particularly the neighborhoods where most of the Act 60 community is concentrated — is genuinely cosmopolitan. There is a growing community of founders, investors, and financial professionals who have made the same move I did. The quality of the professional network available on the island has increased substantially over the past several years, and continues to grow. The conversations I have at a dinner in Condado or Miramar are not materially different from the conversations I would have at a conference in Miami or New York.

The quality of life advantages are real. The climate is exceptional. The cost of living, relative to the major U.S. financial centers, is meaningfully lower. And the ability to step away from the intensity of a major city without actually losing access to everything a major city provides — flights to New York are three and a half hours — is something I have come to value more than I expected.

Building Global from the Island

There is a common assumption that serious global business has to be conducted from certain cities. New York. London. Singapore. The idea that you could run a firm with clients and counterparties across six major stock exchanges from San Juan, Puerto Rico would have struck some of my contacts as eccentric when I first made the move.

It strikes very few of them that way now.

The pandemic-era shift toward remote work permanently recalibrated what it means to be "present" in a market. The tools for staying connected — video conferencing, digital deal rooms, real-time communication platforms — are the same wherever you are. What matters is the quality of your thinking, the depth of your relationships, and the speed of your execution. None of those things require a specific zip code.

A Recommendation

If you are a founder or professional in financial services, consulting, or any business where your value is intellectual rather than geographically bound, I would encourage you to take Puerto Rico seriously. Not as a tax strategy in isolation, but as a holistic decision about where to build something.

Do the research. Engage a qualified tax and legal advisor who specializes in Act 60 compliance. Spend time on the island before making any commitments. Talk to people who have made the move.

What you'll likely find is what I found: a place that makes the economics of running a professional services business better, the quality of your daily life better, and — perhaps most importantly — the breadth of your professional network and perspective significantly wider than you expected.

That combination is rarer than it looks.
