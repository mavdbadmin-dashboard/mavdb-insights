Cross-Border Listings: What Australian, UK, and Canadian Companies Need to Know Before Coming to Nasdaq

By Josh Bartch, Founder & CEO, MavDB Consulting LLC

Most of the companies I work with arrive with the same assumption: that a Nasdaq listing is essentially an extension of what they've already done on their home exchange. They've gone through a listing process before. They understand what investors want. They have a working relationship with market-facing advisors. How different can the U.S. be?

The answer — consistently, without exception — is: more different than you think.

I am not saying this to be discouraging. The U.S. market is worth the complexity. The depth of capital, the liquidity, the global credibility that comes with a Nasdaq listing — these are real advantages that justify the investment of time and resources the process requires. But the companies that navigate it most effectively are the ones who go in with clear eyes about what they're actually dealing with.

Here is what I want companies from Australia, the United Kingdom, and Canada to understand before they begin.

The Exchanges Are Not Equivalent

This sounds obvious, but the implications run deeper than most international executives initially grasp. The ASX, LSE, and TSX/CSE each have their own listing standards, disclosure regimes, investor cultures, and market microstructures. Nasdaq has its own — and the differences are material.

From an ASX perspective, Australian companies are often accustomed to a disclosure-forward regulatory environment with strong continuous disclosure obligations. That rigor is genuinely valuable preparation for a U.S. listing. However, ASX-listed companies frequently have equity structures — particularly the use of performance rights, options, and convertible securities — that don't map cleanly onto U.S. investor expectations. American investors, particularly institutional investors, have specific preferences about how dilution events are structured and disclosed. What's standard on the ASX can look exotic on Nasdaq.

From an LSE or AIM perspective, UK companies need to understand that the SEC's disclosure requirements, while sharing philosophical DNA with the UK's regulatory framework, are considerably more granular in practice. The level of detail required in a Form 20-F or F-1 registration statement — every material contract, every related-party relationship, every risk factor articulated in plain English — often exceeds what UK companies are used to producing. The process of preparing a complete SEC filing frequently surfaces gaps in documentation that companies didn't know existed.

From a Canadian perspective, TSX and CSE-listed companies have perhaps the closest regulatory kinship to the U.S., particularly given the Multijurisdictional Disclosure System (MJDS) that allows certain Canadian issuers to use Canadian documents for some U.S. filings. But the MJDS pathway comes with its own conditions and limitations, and U.S. investors still expect a level of active market engagement — regular analyst calls, roadshows, U.S.-facing investor relations — that many Canadian companies haven't built.

What U.S. Investors Actually Expect

U.S. investors — particularly the institutional investors who will anchor your Nasdaq shareholder base — have developed a specific set of expectations about how public companies communicate, disclose, and engage. Understanding these expectations is not a nice-to-have. It is foundational to building the kind of U.S. investor relationships that sustain long-term value.

Narrative clarity. American investors want a simple, clear story. What does the company do? What is the market opportunity? Why does this management team win? The precision and economy of a good U.S. investor narrative is often a significant departure from the more detailed, technical documents that Australian, UK, and Canadian companies are accustomed to producing. Less is more, if the less is exactly right.

Quarterly rhythm. The U.S. market operates on a quarterly disclosure cycle that structures the entire investor relations calendar. Companies need to be prepared to engage proactively with their investor base every 90 days — earnings releases, shareholder letters, management calls. International companies that are accustomed to semi-annual or annual reporting cycles need to build the operational infrastructure to support this cadence before they list.

Proactive engagement. U.S. institutional investors expect management to come to them. Roadshows, conference presentations, one-on-one meetings — the proactive investor outreach culture in the U.S. is more intensive than in most other markets. Companies that wait for investors to find them will struggle to build the institutional ownership that drives sustainable stock performance.

Regulatory Preparation: The Non-Negotiables

There are several regulatory requirements that international companies must address before a Nasdaq listing, regardless of their home exchange. These are not negotiable, and they take longer than most companies expect.

PCAOB-registered audit. Your financials must be audited by a firm registered with the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board. Many international audit firms are not registered. Switching auditors mid-stream is painful. Start this process early.

GAAP or IFRS as adopted by the SEC. The SEC accepts financial statements prepared under U.S. GAAP or IFRS as adopted by the IASB. If your financials are prepared under a local GAAP that differs from these standards, restatement will be required. This is one of the most time-consuming elements of the preparation process.

Corporate governance standards. Nasdaq's listing standards include specific requirements for board composition, committee structure, and internal controls. Independent directors, audit committees, compensation committees — these requirements need to be in place before you list, not assembled on the fly.

The Advisor Relationship: Why It Matters More Here

In most markets, advisory relationships are important. In cross-border listings, they are mission-critical.

The process of bringing a company from the ASX, LSE, or CSE to Nasdaq involves coordinating across multiple regulatory regimes, multiple legal jurisdictions, multiple investor communities, and often multiple time zones simultaneously. The advisor who manages this process needs to have genuine experience doing it — not theoretical familiarity, but real transactions with real outcomes.

We are involved from the earliest stages of preparation — helping clients understand what they'll need to do and sequencing the work correctly — through the post-listing period when the ongoing investor relations relationship needs to be established and maintained.

The right advisor doesn't just know the rules. They know the investors, the regulators, the investment banks, and the service providers who need to come together to make a cross-border listing succeed. That network is, ultimately, what you are hiring.

Timing and Market Conditions

Finally, a word on timing. The U.S. equity markets are dynamic, and the window for a successful new listing opens and closes with conditions that are partly foreseeable and partly not. Sector rotation, risk appetite, the macro environment, and the pipeline of competing listings all affect how receptive the market will be to a new entrant.

This doesn't mean you should wait for a perfect moment — it doesn't exist. But it does mean that the timing of your listing should be a deliberate strategic decision, informed by real market intelligence and calibrated to your company's specific readiness. Companies that rush the process to hit a market window they're not prepared for almost always regret it. Companies that take the time to be genuinely ready, and then move decisively when conditions are favorable, almost always succeed.

That judgment — when is ready, and when is the window — is one of the most valuable things an experienced advisor brings to the table.
