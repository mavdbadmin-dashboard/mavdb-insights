The Uplisting Playbook: How International Companies Successfully List on Nasdaq

By Josh Bartch, Founder & CEO, MavDB Consulting LLC

Over the years, I've watched a lot of international companies approach a Nasdaq listing the same way — with ambition, strong fundamentals, and a plan that underestimates nearly everything about what's actually required. They're not wrong to be ambitious. A Nasdaq listing can be genuinely transformative: it expands your investor base, increases your liquidity profile, enhances your brand credibility with institutional capital, and opens doors to U.S.-denominated financing that simply aren't available from an overseas exchange. The opportunity is real.

But so is the complexity. And the companies that get it right are almost always the ones that treated the uplisting process as a serious strategic initiative — not a box to check.

Here is what I've learned from advising companies through this process, repeatedly, across multiple exchanges and multiple market cycles.

Why Nasdaq, Specifically?

Before getting into mechanics, it's worth being clear about why Nasdaq has become the destination of choice for so many international companies. The answer is multifaceted.

Nasdaq is home to more than 3,500 companies across virtually every industry, with a particular concentration in technology, biotech, and growth-oriented businesses. Its name carries global brand recognition that translates directly into investor confidence — institutional investors worldwide know what a Nasdaq listing means, and they trust the regulatory standards behind it. For a company based in Australia, the UK, or Canada, a Nasdaq listing signals a commitment to U.S. capital markets standards that domestic exchanges simply cannot replicate.

There's also the liquidity argument. U.S. markets — Nasdaq in particular — offer depth of trading volume that few markets outside the U.S. can match. For a company whose shares have been thinly traded on a regional exchange, the uplift in daily volume that can come with a successful Nasdaq listing is not a small thing. It changes your cost of capital, your ability to raise equity, and your profile with institutional investors who require minimum liquidity thresholds before they can take a position.

What Companies Need to Prepare

The first thing I tell every prospective client is this: start earlier than you think you need to. A well-executed Nasdaq uplisting typically takes 12 to 18 months from the point of serious preparation to the first day of trading. Many take longer. The companies that rush the process are the ones that end up with delayed filings, surprised investors, and market debuts that underperform.

Here's what the preparation actually involves:

Corporate structure and governance. U.S. investors — and Nasdaq's listing standards — have specific expectations about how a company is governed. That means independent directors, properly constituted audit and compensation committees, and financial reporting that meets or exceeds U.S. standards. International companies often need to restructure their boards and formalize governance processes that may have been more informal at home. This takes time, and it should happen before the SEC filing process begins.

Financial audits and SEC-compliant reporting. If your financials are not already being prepared under U.S. GAAP or IFRS as adopted by the SEC, you'll need to restate them. You'll need audited financials for at least two years (sometimes three, depending on your filing category), prepared by a PCAOB-registered audit firm. This is non-negotiable and often the longest lead-time item in the entire process.

Equity structure. Many international companies come to us with capitalization structures that work perfectly well at home but create complications in U.S. markets. Warrants with non-standard terms, complex anti-dilution provisions, or share classes that don't map cleanly onto U.S. investor expectations all need to be addressed before the listing. Getting this right requires careful analysis and, often, negotiation with existing security holders.

Investor relations readiness. A Nasdaq listing is a marketing exercise as much as a regulatory one. Before you ring the bell, you need a compelling investor narrative — a clear articulation of who you are, what market you're in, why you win, and why now. That narrative needs to be built, tested, and delivered through the right channels to the right investors before and during the listing process.

Common Mistakes

I've seen the same mistakes made repeatedly. Here are the most costly:

Underestimating the SEC disclosure process. The level of detail required in a U.S. registration statement is substantially greater than what most international companies are used to. Every material contract, every related-party transaction, every risk factor needs to be disclosed and described. Companies that approach this as a translation exercise — just reformat our existing disclosure — are usually in for a rude awakening.

Ignoring the investor relations build-up. A listing is not the end of the investor relations process. It's the beginning. Companies that don't start building relationships with U.S. investors well before the listing date often find themselves with a weak debut — technically listed, but without the institutional sponsorship that drives sustained trading activity and price performance.

Misjudging market timing. Markets have windows. The difference between a listing that lands in a receptive market environment and one that hits during a period of volatility or sector rotation can be substantial. Getting the timing right requires ongoing market intelligence and the judgment to move when conditions are favorable — even if that means adjusting your original schedule.

Choosing the wrong advisors. This is, predictably, something I care about. The reality is that most advisory firms don't have genuine cross-border experience. They may have done a few international transactions, but they don't have the deep network, the regulatory fluency, or the investor relationships to do this work well. The cost of choosing the wrong advisor is not just the fee — it's the deals you don't get done and the mistakes you make on the way.

The Advisor's Role

The right advisor is not a vendor. They are an extension of your management team for the duration of the listing process. They should have relationships with the SEC staff, with the investment banks who will support your listing, with the institutional investors who will form your initial U.S. shareholder base, and with the legal and accounting professionals who need to execute the technical components of the transaction.

At MavDB, we function as the quarterback of the uplisting process — coordinating across all workstreams, keeping the timeline on track, and bringing our own network to bear at every stage. We've seen enough of these transactions to know where the pressure points are and how to navigate them.

The Timeline in Practice

Here's a rough framework for how a well-managed uplisting process typically unfolds:

- Months 1–3: Structural assessment, governance review, advisor and accounting firm selection, preliminary SEC consultation - Months 4–8: Financial audit and restatement (if needed), registration statement drafting, investor narrative development, pre-listing investor outreach - Months 9–12: SEC review process, roadshow preparation, investor targeting, exchange coordination - Months 12–18: SEC clearance, final investor education, listing and trading commencement

These timelines compress or extend depending on the complexity of the company's structure and the state of U.S. markets. But the framework holds.

The companies that navigate this process successfully are the ones that take it seriously from the start, surround themselves with the right expertise, and execute with discipline. The rewards — access to U.S. capital, enhanced global profile, and the credibility that comes with a Nasdaq listing — are substantial. They're worth doing right.
